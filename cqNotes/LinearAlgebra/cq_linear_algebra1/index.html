Linear transformations. Generally stretches or squishes space. How much are areas scaled?

This special scaling factor, the factor by which the linear transformation changes any area, is called the determinant of the area. The determinant of a transformation would be one half if it squishes the area down to one half, as shown below:

The determinant of a transformation is 0 if it squishes everything into a line, any region would become 0.

Determinant can be negative. Flipping the space. For example, before the transformation, j hat is on the left of i hat, but after the transformation, it is on the right, the orientation has been reversed. Whenever the orientation of the space is inverted, the determinant would be negative. e.g., -3, the space is flipped over, and the area is scaled by a factor of 3.

Chapter 14 Eigenvectors and eigenvalues

Some vectors will get knocked off their span during transformations, where the vector landed were not on that same line.

But some vectors do remain on their span. Meaning that the effect the matrix has on such vector is just to stretch or squish it, like a scalar.

These special vectors (remain on their span) are called the eigenvectors of the transformation (all the vector on that line is stretched by 3 or 2). And each eigenvector has associated with it what’s called an eigenvalue, which is the factor by which it’s squished or squeezed during the transformation. Negative eigenvalue is that the span is flipped.

Finding the transformation of the vector v is the same of finding the solution of lambda and v that make this expression true.

A single eigenvalue can have more than a line full of eigenvectors. For example, scale everything in a coordinate by 2, every vector in the plane will be a eigenvector with eigenvalue 2.
